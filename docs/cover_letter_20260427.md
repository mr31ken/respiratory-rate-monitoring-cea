We wish to submit for your consideration an original research article entitled **"Economic Evaluation of Automated Respiratory Rate Monitoring in General Hospital Wards: A Deterministic Cost-Minimization and Break-Even Modeling Study"** for publication in the *Journal of Clinical Monitoring and Computing*.

Despite respiratory rate being the most heavily weighted vital sign in the National Early Warning Score 2, manual measurement remains widely inaccurate—introducing bias into deterioration detection on general hospital wards. Automated continuous respiratory rate monitoring technologies have proliferated, but no published economic analysis has compared multiple technologies across multiple healthcare systems within a single, transparent, reproducible framework. Our study addresses this gap.

We constructed a deterministic decision-analytic model (CHEERS 2022-compliant) comparing manual respiratory rate counting with six automated monitoring scenarios, parameterized using device accuracy and cost data from Japan, the United Kingdom, Australia, and the United States. The principal contributions are: (1) a unified break-even framework that quantifies the conditions under which device adoption achieves cost-neutrality; (2) demonstration that break-even thresholds (0.24–5.93 avoided deterioration events per 1,000 patient-days) fall within published ward deterioration rates even under worst-case assumptions; and (3) full public release of all input parameters, analysis code, and reproducibility documentation, enabling institutional adaptation with local cost data. To our knowledge, this is the first multi-technology, multi-country economic model for ward-level respiratory rate monitoring.

We believe this manuscript aligns directly with the editorial focus of the *Journal of Clinical Monitoring and Computing*, which sits at the intersection of monitoring technology evaluation and clinical decision support. Our work should be of practical interest to clinicians, biomedical engineers, and hospital decision-makers considering deployment of continuous monitoring on general wards. We deliberately frame our findings as a *threshold analysis*—not a definitive cost-effectiveness conclusion—since randomized evidence on adverse-event reduction remains limited; this conservative framing is consistent with the journal's emphasis on methodological rigor.
